Greek Theatre

By Lukas King

What is greek theatre?

Approximately 2300 years ago between 550 BC and 220 BC, the ancient Greeks created the first drama productions ever. These productions had stemmed from festivals focused on worshipping the Greek god, Dionysus. Greek plays would often fall into two main categories: comedy, and tragedy. As you may be able to infer, a comedy play primarily focuses on humour whereas a tragedy has very sad/dark themes.

These two masks are commonly used to represent comedy and tragedy within theatre.

Where were these plays performed?

Traditional Greek plays were performed at large amphitheatres. In the centre of the stadium, there was a flat, circular stage called the orchestra. This part was for singers and dancers to accompany the main play, which was performed on a raised platform by a group of actors. All of this could be watched by the audience on the raised seats of the stadium, officially called a koilon.

Diagram of a traditional Greek theatre.

What techniques were used?

To enhance the viewing experience, many techniques and tricks were used to improve the clarity of the performance. For example, actors wore padded clothing to increase visibility of the characters, they also wore raised shoes for the same reason and masks were worn to emphasise facial expressions. Techniques in the design of the amphitheatre include sloped seats which projects the actor's voice and raised stages which, yet again, is meant to improve the visibility of the characters.

An example of a real play that was performed.

The story of Oedipus was a tragedy written by Sophocles in which the character Oedipus inadvertently fulfills a prophecy that states he would kill his father and marry his mother. When his mother finds out that Oedipus (her son AND husband) killed his father, she commits suicide and Oedipus gouges his eyes out with pins from his mother's dress, upon learning this tragic information.
